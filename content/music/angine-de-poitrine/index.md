I spend most of my working hours using generative AI to write code, draft documents, and generally smooth out the rough edges of my professional life. Large language models are, by design, optimization machines - they are trained to find the statistical center of mass of human output and produce something that sits comfortably in the middle of the distribution. They are extraordinarily good at this. They are also, by the same token, constitutionally incapable of doing what Angine de Poitrine does.

This post is a genuine expression of my admiration for the band. The irony, though, is real and worth naming upfront: I used AI tools to help write it - to aggregate nerdy facts, trace gear, cross-reference influences, and pull together what other writers and musicians have said about them. Most of what you'll find below is a distillation of excellent work by other authors; I've tried to point back to all of it in the [sources](#sources) at the bottom. My own thoughts are in there too, but the research scaffolding was very much machine-assisted.

Make of that what you will.

## The KEXP session

If you haven't seen it yet, here it is. Everything else I write below will make more sense after you've watched it.

This was recorded live in Rennes, France, during **Trans Musicales** in December 2025 - in front of a room full of art students who were drawing the band as they performed. KEXP uploaded it on February 5, 2026. Within 48 hours it had 400,000 views. By late March: 7.5 million on YouTube, 6 million more on Instagram. KEXP's programming director Morgan Chosnyk said: *"I saw a video of them on my first pass through the lineup and I was like 'Oh yeah, we're definitely booking this band.'"* Anthony Fantano commented *"That's the stuff."* The top YouTube comment, which I cannot improve on, reads: *"This is the only way we can win the battle against AI. Live music forever."*

## Who they are

**Angine de Poitrine** is a duo from **Chicoutimi, in the Saguenay region of Quebec, Canada**. The two members perform as **Khn de Poitrine** (guitar/bass/looper) and **Klek de Poitrine** (drums). Real names unknown - they are committed to keeping it that way, and that's exactly as it should be.

They've played together since they were 13. The band itself formed in 2019 almost by accident - a friend who managed a venue had a slot to fill, they'd already played there that week under a different project, so they invented the masked costumes to disguise themselves. *"The costumes were initially a joke,"* Klek told Noize Magazine. They kept the joke going for about seven years, and here we are.

Their self-description: **"Orchestre Rock Microtonal Dada-Pythago-Cubiste"**. Their booking bio from London's Earth Agency: *"Disciples of planet Earth's rock deities, space-time voyagers Klek and Khn de Poitrine gaze in wonder at hot dogs, pyramids, and rock music in all its glorious excess. Asymmetrical and dissonant, Angine de Poitrine's music makes hearts race and bodies move with ecstatic abandon."* In 2025, they won **Artist of the Year at the Gala alternatif de la musique indépendante du Québec (GAMIQ)**. Since going viral they have declined all record label offers.

## The costumes (yes, this matters)

Full-body black-and-white polka-dot suits. Oversized papier-mâché masks covering their entire heads, with small eye slits cut out - they can barely see their instruments. Khn wears a long golden rope wig. Klek's entire drum kit is draped in a matching polka-dot sheet. Before every set, they face each other with fingers connected to form triangles - JazzTimes described it as *"like sending a radio transmission, or making a salutation before a kung fu match."* They communicate on stage in an invented alien language.

The anonymity is intentional beyond the aesthetic. Klek described it as *"an interesting philosophical exercise... a social experiment, a challenge to ideas of success and validation from people who don't know you."* Meanwhile the people in those suits are playing one of the most technically demanding live setups in rock music today - while wearing masks with eye slits, in venues that are presumably quite warm.

## The sound

### The instrument

Khn plays a **custom double-necked microtonal guitar/bass** - one neck for guitar (Stratocaster-style body), one for bass (Precision-style body), joined together. The instrument was commissioned from luthier **Raphaël Le Breton** of Lutherie Le Breton in Alma, Quebec, who built it after being contacted by the band in July 2023. What matters most is the fretboard: **twice as many frets as a standard guitar**, enabling quarter-tone intervals between every standard note.

Here's the origin story: Klek built the first microtonal guitar himself. *"I added more frets on a guitar, with a saw,"* he told Noize Magazine. He then handed it to Khn. This is the kind of origin story that sounds made up but absolutely is not.

Khn's stated goal with the quarter tones is to **use them as full vocabulary members, not as embellishments**. Most Western microtonal experimentation (and a lot of Eastern music as heard through a Western lens) treats the notes between the standard 12 as color - a spice, an inflection. Khn does something different:

> *"My idea was definitely to see how to get that kind of slightly Arabic-sounding colour. But I quickly moved towards a more modal approach, to try and exploit the potential for dissonance it offers, without falling into things that are very stereotypical and reminiscent of a culture that, ultimately, isn't mine. My musical background is very much rooted in progressive rock, modern jazz, and contemporary music. It's just natural for me to use quarter tones in a language that's more cubist, let's say, more Zappa-esque in its phrasing. The way I use quarter tones allows me to explore chromatic approaches that are twice as long and then build more tension. But ultimately, most of the tunes we make could be harmonically compared to something along the lines of the tunes on John Scofield's Überjam or Miles Davis's So What."*
>
> — Khn de Poitrine, [Noize Magazine](https://www.noizemagazine.com/blog/angine-de-poitrine-a-discussion-with-the-infectious-quebec-band)

That last comparison is key to why this music is actually listenable. The harmonic language is grounded in jazz-modal thinking - *So What* is famously approachable despite being modal and relatively static harmonically. The quarter tones add texture and dissonance without the music ever losing its center of gravity. It sounds foreign and familiar at the same time.

Guitar influences Khn has named: **Frank Zappa** (phrasing approach), **Leo Brouwer** (classical guitar), **John Scofield** (modern jazz), **Arthur Lindsay of the Lounge Lizards** (*"He never plays a clearly definable note or a melody. I love Arthur Lindsay's guitar playing!"*), and **René Lussier** (Quebec avant-garde). JazzTimes editor David R. Adler also drew the comparison to **Dave "Fuze" Fiuczynski**, who has been one of the few other guitarists working seriously in microtonal rock.

The broader sonic reference points reviewers have reached for: **Primus** + microtonal **King Gizzard & the Lizard Wizard** + **Buckethead** + **The White Stripes** (In Spite Magazine). **Zs, Mick Barr, Domi & JD Beck** (JazzTimes). **Death from Above 1979 on mushrooms** (a YouTube comment that I think is the most accurate description available). King Gizzard's *Flying Microtonal Banana* is apparently a direct and acknowledged influence - one of the only mainstream rock records that took the instrument seriously.

### The looper and pedalboard

The **Boss RC-600 Loop Station** is what turns a two-piece into a full sonic architecture. Khn used the RC-500 on the 2024 tour; the KEXP session used the RC-600. The rest of the pedalboard, documented through Instagram stories and the *Pedalboards of Doom* Facebook community (yes, this exists):

**Boss TU-3** (tuner) - **Boss DB-90 Dr. Beat** (metronome, which implies things about the precision required) - **MXR M288 Bass Octave Deluxe** - **Pro Co RAT 2** - **Mad Professor Supreme** (overdrive) - **Fender Engager Boost** - **Warm Audio RingerBringer** (ring modulator, controlled by expression pedals) - **Radial BigShot ABY** (switcher for the dual necks) - **Pike Amplification Vulcan XL** - **Boss DD-8 Digital Delay**. Guitar World noted that Reddit has entire threads devoted to identifying everything on that board.

Khn on what the looper actually does to the music:

> *"Our musical concept is definitely based on [the looper], to the point that it becomes an instrument in itself. For me, it's a demanding task in terms of mastering the tool... it creates really significant limitations, not only in terms of arrangements, but also in the song's structure... What I find interesting is that it inevitably leads us into an aesthetic territory somewhat reminiscent of techno music."*
>
> — Khn de Poitrine, [Noize Magazine](https://www.noizemagazine.com/blog/angine-de-poitrine-a-discussion-with-the-infectious-quebec-band)

And on the hardest part of the whole setup: *"handling the loop pedal while playing the double-necked microtonal guitar"* - while nearly blind inside a papier-mâché mask. The metronome on that pedalboard is not a suggestion.

### Klek's drumming

JazzTimes editor David R. Adler described it best:

> *"Klek's drumming is unfailingly deep in the pocket, never stiff, but rather elastic as it undergirds Khn's layered, superimposed guitar/bass riffs, which shift like fractals, with jagged shapes breaking in all directions. The beats are infectious no matter how abstruse and unstable."*
>
> — David R. Adler, [JazzTimes](https://www.jazztimes.com/features/profiles/the-off-world-aesthetic-of-quebecs-angine-de-poitrine/)

The rhythmic vocabulary sits in the overlap of prog, math rock, and mathcore for the composed sections, over a foundation that PAN M 360 described as "tribal grooves immersed in a thick magma of effects pedals." Klek's stated primary influence is **Gentle Giant** - the 1970s prog band who opened for Black Sabbath and got booed, which is exactly the right reference point for someone who plays like this.

As a guitarist, what gets me most is the looping side of this equation. Playing in odd time signatures is one thing - it's hard, but you practice it until it's internalized. Looping *in* odd time signatures is a different problem entirely: you have to land your loop endpoint with sample-accurate precision on every repetition, in a meter that doesn't resolve where your body expects it to. And once the loop is running, it's fixed - it doesn't breathe, it doesn't adjust. What Klek does on top of that is the part I find genuinely inspiring: he keeps it interesting. The groove doesn't just hold the loop in place, it plays against it, adds layers of feel on top of something mechanically static. That's the thing that separates a technically correct drummer from a musical one - the ability to make a locked loop feel alive rather than like a click track that escaped.

The important thing is that none of this reads as showing off. The odd meters and polyrhythmic patterns lock with Khn's loops in a way that makes the whole thing feel inevitable. It grooves despite being technically absurd.

**And they do this live.** Looper-based music in the studio is a solved problem - you build your loops carefully, you comp them until they're tight, you move on. Live looping is categorically different: every layer has to land on time, every time, while you're also managing the microtonal double-neck, while you can barely see. A single mistimed loop and the whole architecture collapses. The KEXP session was a full live performance, not overdubbed. The room was full of people drawing them. And it sounds like the record, except more alive.

## The AI angle

The thing AI tools cannot do - the thing that is structurally impossible for them to do - is produce something like Angine de Poitrine. Not because AI can't generate music (it can, increasingly well). But because generative models optimize for the center of the distribution, and Angine de Poitrine lives at the tail. Their sound is the product of two people who have played together since they were teenagers, who built their own instruments with a saw, who invented a fake language for stage communication, who have been grinding Quebec festival circuits for years before anyone outside the region noticed. The KEXP video comment that stuck with me wasn't hyperbole: *"This is the only way we can win the battle against AI. Live music forever."*

I don't think it's a battle. But I understand the sentiment completely, and I think the impulse behind it - the need to assert that something irreplaceable exists - is worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as technophobia.

What AI tools do well is production at scale: functional output, fast, good enough. What they structurally cannot do is accumulate two decades of shared musical history, build an instrument with a saw, develop a stage persona with an invented language, and then drag all of that into a room in Rennes in front of art students and make it feel like a transmission from another planet. That's not a capability gap that gets closed with more parameters. It's a different category of thing entirely.

The audiences are also just not the same. Someone reaching for an AI-generated playlist as background music for their afternoon is not in competition with someone who just watched the KEXP session three times and is now trying to figure out whether Khn is using a Warm Audio RingerBringer or an original Ring Thing. These are different needs, different relationships to music, different moments. Collapsing them into a single AI-vs-human narrative loses exactly the nuance that makes the conversation worth having - the same way we don't say photography "replaced" painting just because both produce images. Top-tier artistry has always found its audience regardless of what the dominant medium is doing. Angine de Poitrine will be fine. And music as a whole, will be fine.
